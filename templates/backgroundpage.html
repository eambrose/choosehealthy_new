While investigating the impact of food deserts on community health, we noticed that food access isn't the most important contributor to healthy eating. Here's the story:

The poverty level in your community impacts the kind of food you have access to.

The number of full service restaurants dramatically declines relative to fast food restaurants as poverty level increases.

At the same time, the number of convenience stores relative to grocery stores is drastically increased.

... and diabetes

This health discrepancy is not explained by food access or grocery store availability.

I used poverty level, food access, and grocery store availability to predict high or low obesity in a community using logistic regression. Surprisingly, removing food access, grocery store availability (or both) from the model did not decrease model accuracy.


The data shown here is the mean (red dot) and 95% confidence interval (blue line) of prediction accuracy using 10,000 cross validation steps, for the full model and three reduced models after dropping a single predictor variable.

Access isn't everything when it comes to the food people are eating.

There are obviously many factors that influence health which are not included in my models: exercise, family history, and access to healthcare to name a few, as well as many demographic differences which influence food access.
